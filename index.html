Project Introduction

This project explores air pollution data from the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency to analyze how air quality varies across time, geographic regions, and pollutant types. By identifying patterns and differences in pollution levels, this project highlights how environmental conditions are shaped by location, infrastructure, and human activity. These insights can provide a foundation for future research linking pollution exposure to public health outcomes.

Data Description

The dataset is sourced from the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency and accessed through CSV files and APIs. It includes daily pollution measurements across multiple locations and pollutants. Key attributes include date, AQI, pollutant type, concentration levels, observation counts, and geographic information such as city, state, latitude, and longitude.

This structure enables analysis of temporal trends, geographic comparisons, and pollutant-specific patterns, making it well-suited for understanding air quality dynamics at multiple scales.

Data Visualizations

CO and AQI Trends Over Time

This visualization tracks carbon monoxide (CO) levels and AQI in Boston across the year. Both variables move closely together, with noticeable fluctuations and spikes occurring throughout the timeline rather than within a specific season.

Key Insight: CO and AQI show a strong relationship, suggesting that carbon monoxide plays a significant role in overall air quality. Pollution levels fluctuate frequently, indicating short-term variability rather than smooth seasonal trends.

AQI Distribution by Area Type

This boxplot compares AQI distributions across coastal, rural, suburban, and urban areas, highlighting differences in variability and extreme values.

Key Insight: Urban areas show higher and more variable AQI, while coastal areas remain cleaner and more stable. However, all area types experience occasional pollution spikes.

Median CO Levels by Region

This bar chart compares median carbon monoxide concentrations across U.S. regions, allowing for consistent comparison while reducing the impact of outliers.

Key Insight: Southern regions exhibit higher baseline pollution levels, likely due to population density and industrial activity, while Alaska and Hawaii remain lower.

CO Trends Across Major Cities

This scatter plot compares daily CO levels across major U.S. cities, revealing differences in variability and consistency over time.

Key Insight: Cities show distinct pollution patterns, suggesting that local factors such as traffic, infrastructure, and population density strongly influence air quality.

Multi-Pollutant Trends

This interactive visualization displays multiple pollutant concentrations across the U.S. over time, allowing users to explore patterns across pollutants and locations.

Key Insight: Pollutant concentrations are higher in urban areas and show noticeable increases during certain time periods, reflecting human activity patterns and seasonal effects.

Summary and Future Work

Air pollution varies significantly across time and geography. Urban areas tend to experience higher and more variable pollution levels, while other regions remain relatively stable.

Future work could integrate health data, expand to multiple years, and add deeper interactive exploration to better understand long-term environmental and public health impacts.
