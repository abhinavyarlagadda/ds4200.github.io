Project Introduction

This project will focus on air pollution datasets from the United States Environmental Protection Agency. By exploring and analyzing pollutant data across multiple geographic areas over time, we may be able to glean insights regarding overall trends, seasonal patterns, and the impact of human events, circumstances, and natural phenomena on public health. These insights may then be used in conjunction with public health and healthcare data to deliver informative suggestions to both the government and the public to aid in the improvement of public health and to help mitigate health risks during predictable events.

Data Description

The data is available through two methods: The data is downloadable from the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) as .csv files, and can be further divided by pollutant, year, geographical area, and monitor site (categorical features) if a narrower scope is desired, and there is also an API (also from the EPA) which data can be extracted. There is significant variation in the number of rows available for a specific pollutant and geographical area, but each area will generally have somewhere between 400 and 900 rows for each pollutant (that is monitored) per year (estimated from available data for 2025). Significant features available in the dataset include the following: Date Daily Maximum Daily Air Quality Index (AQI) Pollutant Type Location (Cities, Counties, States) Daily Observation Count Monitor Site ID Depending on the scope of the finalized project, this database can later be used in conjunction with location-divided health data (available from the CDC website) to derive insights into correlations between public health and concentration of pollutants.

Data Visualizations

Visualization 1:

Description: The plot shows how carbon monoxide (CO) levels and AQI change over time in Boston across the year. The two lines track each other closely, with both rising and falling at similar points in time. Instead of a smooth trend, the values fluctuate frequently, with several noticeable spikes throughout the year. These spikes appear in multiple parts of the timeline rather than being concentrated in just one season.

Takeaway: From the plot, it’s clear that CO and AQI move closely together, which suggests that carbon monoxide levels are a strong contributor to overall air quality. The data also shows a lot of short-term fluctuations, meaning pollution levels can change pretty quickly instead of following a smooth trend. There isn’t one specific time of year where everything spikes consistently, so air quality issues seem to happen throughout the year rather than being seasonal. Overall, even though most values stay moderate, the occasional spikes stand out and could be important for identifying short-term pollution events.

Visualization 2:

Description: The boxplot compares how AQI values are distributed across different area types: coastal, rural, suburban, and urban. Each box shows the spread of AQI values within that group, along with how concentrated or spread out the data is. Some groups have tighter distributions, while others show wider variation and more extreme values. Outliers are also visible, indicating occasional unusually high AQI levels in certain areas.

Takeaway: The boxplot shows that urban areas generally have worse and more inconsistent air quality compared to other areas, while coastal areas tend to be cleaner and more stable. Rural and suburban areas fall somewhere in between, but they still experience some spikes, which means no area is completely free from pollution. Another important point is that all groups have outliers, so even places with usually good air quality can still have occasional bad days. Overall, the type of area clearly affects AQI, with urban environments being the most impacted.

Visualization 3:

Description: This scatter plot shows how AQI values vary across monitoring sites at different latitudes. Each point represents one observation, with color used to distinguish pollutant type. The visualization helps reveal whether certain latitude ranges tend to experience higher air quality concerns.

Takeaway: This visualization helps show whether air quality patterns differ by geographic position. If clusters of higher AQI values appear at certain latitudes, it suggests that regional climate, population density, or industrial activity may influence pollution levels.

Visualization 5:

Description: This visualization serves to show the concentrations of multiple parameters (pollutants) over all valid monitor sites across the United States over time. Disclaimer: not all monitoring sites will record the same parameters, and data for the last third of the year is sparse, if not absent due to the time needed to update the data on the public databases. Additionally, this visualization may take some time to load, given the size of the dataset.

Takeaway: This visualization allows users to observe changes in concentration of different pollutants across time in monitor sites across the United States. With industrial plants and mass burning of fossil fuels often taking place in major cities and industrial towns, there is a clear distinction that monitor sites in urban centers measure greater concentrations of pollutants such as NO2 and SO2. While carbon monoxide readings are generally expected, and observed, to be higher in urban areas, in the dimension of time, it is noticeable that in the summer months, maybe most exemplary in the days around July 4th (day=185), carbon monoxide readings are reported to be higher, even significantly higher than in urban centers.

Interaction Features

  • On-hover tooltips display monitor site, date, measurement specifics, and pollutant type.
  • The parameter dropdown allows to filter the dataset to view measurements for one specific pollutant at a time.
  • The time slider allows the user to observe, through colors, trends across periods of time, and pick up patterns temporally and geographically, with lower attention to any one particular point.

Summary and Future Work

This project explored air quality trends across the United States using EPA pollutant data, with a focus on understanding how pollution levels vary over time and across different types of regions. The analysis showed that carbon monoxide and AQI are closely related, indicating that CO is a meaningful contributor to overall air quality. While pollution levels generally remain moderate, there are frequent short-term spikes that suggest air quality can change quickly and unpredictably. Additionally, regional comparisons revealed that urban areas tend to experience higher and more variable AQI levels, while coastal areas are generally cleaner and more stable. Broader visualizations also highlighted geographic differences in pollutant concentrations, with certain regions consistently showing higher levels than others. Overall, the findings emphasize that air pollution is influenced by both location and time, and that even areas with relatively good air quality can still experience occasional high-risk events. Future work could expand this analysis by integrating public health datasets, such as respiratory illness or hospital admission rates, to better understand the real-world impact of pollution exposure. Incorporating additional years of data would also help identify longer-term trends and determine whether air quality is improving or worsening over time. Another potential improvement would be refining geographic classifications or analyzing specific cities in greater detail to uncover more localized patterns. Finally, enhancing interactivity in the visualizations, such as adding filtering by pollutant thresholds, could make the tool more useful for both policymakers and the general public in identifying and responding to pollution risks.
