Project Introduction

This project focuses on air pollution datasets from the United States Environmental Protection Agency. By exploring and analyzing pollutant data across multiple geographic areas over time, this project aims to identify overall trends, regional differences, and patterns in air quality. Understanding these trends can provide useful insight into how location, time, and pollutant type shape environmental conditions. In the future, these findings could also be paired with public health data to better understand how pollution exposure may relate to health risks and outcomes.

Data Description

The data used in this project comes from the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency and is available through downloadable CSV files as well as an API. The dataset can be filtered by pollutant, year, geographic area, and monitor site depending on the scope of the analysis. For a given pollutant and location, the number of observations can vary, but each area generally contains several hundred daily records across the year. Important attributes in the dataset include date, AQI, pollutant type, arithmetic mean concentration, daily maximum values, observation count, and location information such as city, county, state, latitude, and longitude. These variables make it possible to examine air quality over time, compare conditions across regions, and explore broader geographic patterns.

Data Visualizations

This section explores air quality from multiple perspectives, including time trends, geographic variation, regional comparisons, and differences across area types.

Visualization 1:

Description: This line chart shows how carbon monoxide and AQI change over time in Boston across the year. The two lines move closely together, allowing for a direct comparison between pollutant concentration and overall air quality. Rather than following a smooth seasonal pattern, both variables fluctuate frequently, with several visible spikes appearing at different points in the year.

Takeaway: This visualization suggests that carbon monoxide levels and AQI are closely related in Boston, since both rise and fall in similar ways over time. It also shows that air quality does not change smoothly across the year, but instead experiences short-term spikes that may reflect temporary pollution events.

Visualization 2:

Description: This scatter plot shows daily average carbon monoxide concentration levels over time across six major U.S. cities in 2025. Each point represents the average CO level recorded on a given day for a city, with color used to distinguish between cities. The visualization makes it possible to compare overall concentration ranges, variation, and periods of higher pollution across multiple locations at once.

Takeaway: This chart shows that carbon monoxide patterns differ across cities, with some locations showing consistently higher or more variable concentrations than others. It highlights that pollution levels are not uniform across urban areas and that some cities experience more noticeable spikes throughout the year.

Visualization 3:

Description: This boxplot compares how AQI values are distributed across different area types: coastal, rural, suburban, and urban. Each box summarizes the spread of AQI values within that group, showing the middle range, central tendency, and outliers. This makes it easier to compare both typical air quality conditions and overall variability across different types of environments.

Takeaway: This visualization shows that urban areas tend to have higher and less consistent AQI values, while coastal areas appear cleaner and more stable overall. Rural and suburban areas fall in between, but still show occasional high-AQI outliers, which suggests that no area type is completely free from short-term pollution events.

Visualization 4:

Description: This bar chart shows the median daily carbon monoxide concentration across different U.S. regions in 2025. Each bar represents one region, making it easy to compare typical CO levels across the country. Using median values helps reduce the effect of extreme outliers and gives a clearer picture of usual regional conditions.

Takeaway: This chart shows that median carbon monoxide concentrations are not evenly distributed across the United States. Regions such as the Southeast and South Central report higher typical CO levels, while Alaska and Hawaii show the lowest, pointing to broad regional differences in pollution conditions.

Visualization 5:

Description: This visualization serves to show the concentrations of multiple parameters (pollutants) over all valid monitor sites across the United States over time. Disclaimer: not all monitoring sites will record the same parameters, and data for the last third of the year is sparse, if not absent due to the time needed to update the data on the public databases. Additionally, this visualization may take some time to load, given the size of the dataset.

Takeaway: This visualization allows users to observe changes in concentration of different pollutants across time in monitor sites across the United States. With industrial plants and mass burning of fossil fuels often taking place in major cities and industrial towns, there is a clear distinction that monitor sites in urban centers measure greater concentrations of pollutants such as NO2 and SO2. While carbon monoxide readings are generally expected, and observed, to be higher in urban areas, in the dimension of time, it is noticeable that in the summer months, maybe most exemplary in the days around July 4th (day=185), carbon monoxide readings are reported to be higher, even significantly higher than in urban centers.

Interaction Features

  • On-hover tooltips display monitor site, date, measurement specifics, and pollutant type.
  • The parameter dropdown allows to filter the dataset to view measurements for one specific pollutant at a time.
  • The time slider allows the user to observe, through colors, trends across periods of time, and pick up patterns temporally and geographically, with lower attention to any one particular point.

Summary and Future Work

This project analyzes air quality trends across the United States using EPA pollutant data, focusing on how pollution varies over time, geography, and area type. Across the visualizations, several consistent patterns emerge. Carbon monoxide levels and AQI show a strong relationship over time, with both variables moving closely together and exhibiting frequent short-term spikes rather than smooth seasonal trends. Comparisons across area types reveal that urban regions tend to have higher and more variable AQI values, while coastal areas remain relatively stable, suggesting that population density and human activity play a significant role in pollution levels.

City-level and regional comparisons further show that pollution is not evenly distributed, with certain regions such as the Southeast and South Central consistently showing higher carbon monoxide concentrations. The interactive geographic visualization reinforces that air quality varies spatially across monitoring sites, with clusters of higher concentrations appearing in more developed or densely populated areas. Together, these results show that air quality is shaped by a combination of temporal variation, geographic location, and human activity.

Future work could expand this analysis by incorporating public health datasets such as hospital admissions or respiratory illness rates in order to better understand the effects of pollution exposure. Extending the dataset across multiple years would also allow for identification of longer-term trends and whether air quality is improving or worsening over time. More detailed geographic analysis at the city or neighborhood level could reveal local pollution patterns that are not visible at a broader scale. In addition, integrating outside variables such as weather, traffic, or industrial activity could help explain why certain spikes or regional differences appear. From a visualization standpoint, adding more advanced filtering and improving clarity in denser plots would make the project even more useful for both policymakers and the general public.
