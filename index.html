Project Introduction

This project focuses on air pollution datasets from the United States Environmental Protection Agency. By exploring and analyzing pollutant data across multiple geographic areas over time, this project aims to identify overall trends, regional differences, and patterns in air quality. Understanding these trends can provide useful insight into how location, time, and pollutant type shape environmental conditions. In the future, these findings could also be paired with public health data to better understand how pollution exposure may relate to health risks and outcomes.

Data Description

The data used in this project comes from the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency and is available through downloadable CSV files as well as an API. The dataset can be filtered by pollutant, year, geographic area, and monitor site depending on the scope of the analysis. For a given pollutant and location, the number of observations can vary, but each area generally contains several hundred daily records across the year. Important attributes in the dataset include date, AQI, pollutant type, arithmetic mean concentration, daily maximum values, observation count, and location information such as city, county, state, latitude, and longitude. These variables make it possible to examine air quality over time, compare conditions across regions, and explore broader geographic patterns.

Data Visualizations

Visualization 1:

Description: The plot shows how carbon monoxide (CO) levels and AQI change over time in Boston across the year. The two lines track each other closely, with both rising and falling at similar points in time. Instead of a smooth trend, the values fluctuate frequently, with several noticeable spikes throughout the year. These spikes appear in multiple parts of the timeline rather than being concentrated in just one season.

Takeaway: From the plot, it’s clear that CO and AQI move closely together, which suggests that carbon monoxide levels are a strong contributor to overall air quality. The data also shows a lot of short-term fluctuations, meaning pollution levels can change pretty quickly instead of following a smooth trend. There isn’t one specific time of year where everything spikes consistently, so air quality issues seem to happen throughout the year rather than being seasonal. Overall, even though most values stay moderate, the occasional spikes stand out and could be important for identifying short-term pollution events.

Visualization 2:

Description: The boxplot compares how AQI values are distributed across different area types: coastal, rural, suburban, and urban. Each box shows the spread of AQI values within that group, along with how concentrated or spread out the data is. Some groups have tighter distributions, while others show wider variation and more extreme values. Outliers are also visible, indicating occasional unusually high AQI levels in certain areas.

Takeaway: The boxplot shows that urban areas generally have worse and more inconsistent air quality compared to other areas, while coastal areas tend to be cleaner and more stable. Rural and suburban areas fall somewhere in between, but they still experience some spikes, which means no area is completely free from pollution. Another important point is that all groups have outliers, so even places with usually good air quality can still have occasional bad days. Overall, the type of area clearly affects AQI, with urban environments being the most impacted.

Visualization 3:

Description:

Takeaway: This visualization compares median daily CO concentrations across U.S. regions in 2025, revealing that the Southeast and South Central regions consistently recorded the highest carbon monoxide levels while Alaska/Hawaii recorded the lowest.

Visualization 4:

Description: This scatter plot shows daily average carbon monoxide concentration levels over time across six major U.S. cities in 2025. Each point represents the average CO level recorded on a given day for a city, with color used to distinguish between cities. The visualization allows viewers to compare how CO concentrations change over the year and whether some cities experience consistently higher levels or more variation than others.

Takeaway: This visualization highlights differences in carbon monoxide patterns across cities over time. Some cities show more consistent levels while others have greater variability, indicating that local factors such as traffic, population density, or industrial activity may influence pollution levels. It also shows that fluctuations occur throughout the year rather than following a simple seasonal pattern.

Visualization 5:

Description: This visualization serves to show the concentrations of multiple parameters (pollutants) over all valid monitor sites across the United States over time. Disclaimer: not all monitoring sites will record the same parameters, and data for the last third of the year is sparse, if not absent due to the time needed to update the data on the public databases. Additionally, this visualization may take some time to load, given the size of the dataset.

Takeaway: This visualization allows users to observe changes in concentration of different pollutants across time in monitor sites across the United States. With industrial plants and mass burning of fossil fuels often taking place in major cities and industrial towns, there is a clear distinction that monitor sites in urban centers measure greater concentrations of pollutants such as NO2 and SO2. While carbon monoxide readings are generally expected, and observed, to be higher in urban areas, in the dimension of time, it is noticeable that in the summer months, maybe most exemplary in the days around July 4th (day=185), carbon monoxide readings are reported to be higher, even significantly higher than in urban centers.

Interaction Features

  • On-hover tooltips display monitor site, date, measurement specifics, and pollutant type.
  • The parameter dropdown allows to filter the dataset to view measurements for one specific pollutant at a time.
  • The time slider allows the user to observe, through colors, trends across periods of time, and pick up patterns temporally and geographically, with lower attention to any one particular point.

Summary and Future Work

This project explored air quality patterns across the United States using EPA pollutant data, focusing on how pollution varies over time, across regions, and between different types of areas. The visualizations show that carbon monoxide and AQI are closely related, reinforcing that CO plays a key role in overall air quality. While most values remain within moderate ranges, there are frequent short-term spikes, indicating that air quality can change quickly rather than following a smooth or predictable trend.

Comparisons across geographic areas reveal clear differences. Urban environments tend to have higher and more variable pollution levels, while coastal areas are generally cleaner and more stable. City-level analysis further highlights that pollution patterns are not uniform, with some cities experiencing more variability than others. These differences suggest that local conditions such as traffic, infrastructure, and population density have a strong influence on air quality.

Overall, the project shows that air pollution is both location-dependent and time-dependent, and that even areas with relatively good air quality can still experience occasional spikes that may impact public health.

For future work, this analysis could be expanded by incorporating public health data, such as hospital visits or respiratory illness rates, to better understand the real-world effects of pollution exposure. Including multiple years of data would also help identify long-term trends and determine whether air quality is improving or worsening over time. Additionally, refining geographic breakdowns or focusing more deeply on individual cities could provide more detailed insights. Finally, adding more interactive features would allow users to explore the data more dynamically and make the visualizations more useful for decision-making.
